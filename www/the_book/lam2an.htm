Lamentations 2 - Outline of Lamentations (MENU page)
The Lord's Actions against Jerusalem, due to His Righteous Anger, ch. 2
Jeremiah's first lamentation (ch. 1), described the desolation of the city, identified the cause of that desolation ("Jerusalem hath grievously sinned," 1:8, ..."the LORD is righteous," 1:18), and expressed unspeakable sorrow under the weight and consequences of that sin: "Behold and see if there be any sorrow like unto my sorrow, wherewith the LORD hath afflicted me in the day of his fierce anger" (1:12). It is the unimaginable thought of that last line upon which this second lamentation dwells. The LORD, Jehovah, the God who established and keeps His covenant with Israel, has caused her sorrow.
I. The Lord's Actions against Jerusalem, 2:1-10
  1. were motivated by His anger [The word occurs twice in this verse.], v.1
    • He covered them with a cloud (ie., of access denied, Lam 3:44; and of gloom and doom, Joel 2:2)
    • He cast down... the beauty of Israel (ie., the Temple, Eze 7:20-22)
    • He remembered not His footstool (ie., the Ark of the Covenant and its resting place, 1Chr 28:2; Psa 132:1-10)
  2. against her habitations (the humble houses of shepherds and the strong fortresses of kings, alike), v.2a
  3. against her political power, v.2b-3
    • He has cut off her horn {ie., power}, cp. Psa 75:5,10; Psa 132:13-18
    • He has polluted {ie., made profane or common, removed the honor of} the kingdom and its princes.
    • He has drawn back His right hand (with which He would have defended them, cp. Psa 74:10,11)
  4. against her people, v.4,5 - cp. Isa 63:10; Jer 21:5
    • He assaults and prevails against all, regardless of whether they dwell in tents or palaces.
    • He swallowed up {HB= bala; engulfed completely} Israel and the place of her abode.
      [The word "bala" occurs in v.2, 5 (2x), 8 ("destroying"). It conveys the picture of a fire consuming everything (v.3). cp. Jer 52:13]
  5. against His Temple, v.6,7
    • He has violently taken away His Tabernacle (ie., the Temple).
      The word used for Tabernacle here is HB= sok, which may be related to the HB word "sukah" meaning "booth," or to the HB word "suwk" meaning "hedge").
           If the meaning is "booth," the Lord has removed the place of meeting {assembly} which He had established in the midst of His people, as easily as a farmer removes a temporary shelter used for shade, when the harvest is ended (cp. Isa 1:8).
           If the meaning is "hedge," the Lord has turned the garden that He planted back to its natural state (cp. Psa 80:12; 89:40; Isa 5:5).
    • He hath cast off His altar... He hath abhorred His sanctuary...- cp. Isa 1:13-15; Jer 7:12-14; Their religious activity was not according to His will. The church should take warning also (cp. Rev 2:4,5).
    • He hath given up into the hand of the enemy... - They (the enemy) make a noise in the Lord's House, as in the former days of Israel's feasts. But this is the sound, not of worship, but of demolition.
  6. against her wall (ie., defenses, protection), v.8,9a
    • He hath stretched out a line...- ie., a plumb line. That which was not upright must be torn down. cp. 2Kin 21:13; Isa 28:17; 34:11; Amos 7:7,8
    • He hath made the rampart... wall... gates... to lament {ie., mourn their failure}, languish {ie., grow weak} and sink into the ground. The physical barriers to the enemy are broken down.
  7. against her rulers (leaders), v.9b,10a
    • The kings, princes, prophets, elders...- the defenders of the Law, have failed, and do not hear from God.
  8. resulting in her sorrow, v.10b
Additional notes on this section:
  • The many strong verbs that describe these actions show that the Lord Himself has purposely done these things (eg., "The Lord was as an enemy: he hath swallowed up... he hath destroyed... he hath increased [her] mourning and lamentation." v.5)
  • In v.1-5, it is "the Lord" {Adonai, master, ruler, husband, the One in authority} who takes these actions against His rebellious servants.
  • In v.6-10, it is "the LORD" {Jehovah, the self existent One} who chose this people and this place for the glory of His Name, who violently takes away the garden {or, enclosed garden, cp. Song 4:12} that He had planted for Himself.
II. The resulting sorrow of Jeremiah: The lament of an eyewitness, v.11-19
Jeremiah was broken hearted and broken in health, having been overwhelmed with sorrow. The "bowels" and "liver" were regarded as seats of the emotions and sympathies (cp. Jer 4:19). But strong emotions also have physical effects, such as tears, nausea and diarrhea (cp. Job 16:13-16).
  1. for the children, v.11,12
  2. for his inability to comfort his people, v.13 - Their wound was beyond curing (cp. Jer 8:22; 30:12).
  3. for the error of false prophets, v.14 -
    • They had not discovered {ie., unveiled, revealed} the iniquity which led to their captivity.
    • Their false burdens, which contradicted God's Word through true prophets, seduced the people into destruction. (The word "banishment" is lit. "seduction.")
  4. for the mockery of their enemies, v.15-17
    • Is this... The perfection of beauty... The joy of the whole earth... - cp. Psa 48:2; 50:2; Isa 64:11; Deu 29:22-28
    • We have swallowed her up {HB= bala}... - Israel's enemies take credit for what the Lord Himself has done (v.5).
    v.17 is a summary statement, reminding that the LORD, not their enemies, had caused their sorrows:
    • He hath done what He devised {purposed, planned}, including that which befell the children (cp. v.11,12,20,21; Lev 26:27-29; Deu 28:53-57).
    • He hath fulfilled His Word, in spite of the conflicting message of the popular prophets (cp. v.14)
    • He hath thrown down, and not pitied - Therefore, comfort was futile (cp. v.13).
    • He hath caused thine enemy to rejoice over thee... and established their horn {their power} (cp. v.15,16).
  5. for the despairing wail of the people toward the Lord, v.18-22
    1. Jeremiah observes some of the people praying (v.18)-
      • appealing to the Lord {Adonai} as their wall {protector, defender}, to remember His people, the apple of His eye (cp. Deu 32:10; Psa 17:8; Zech 2:8), and to intercede for them. (Read v.18 with the supplied word: "Their heart cried unto the Lord, saying...")
    2. Jeremiah encourages the people to pray (v.19)-
      • motivated to fervent prayer by the plight of their children, they were to pour out their soul before the Lord (cp. Psa 62:8; 142:2; according to Jeremiah's example, v.11).
    3. Jeremiah reports (or suggests) the substance of their prayer:
      1. Their Appeal to the LORD,
        • Consider to whom thou hast done this (ie., to the apple of thine eye), v.20
        • Consider our desperate condition, v.21
          • children are being cannibalized.
          • dead bodies lie discarded as refuse, indiscriminate of age, gender, or position. (The children who escape being eaten by their parents, are consumed by the enemy. v.22b)
      2. Their Acknowledgment that their bereavement is due to LORD's anger, v.22
    What sorrows must the Lord bring upon His people before we seek Him as we ought? cp. 2Chr 7:14; Isa 55:6-13; Jer 29:12-14
    How much better to seek Him early? Psa 63:1; 1Tim 2:8
